Implementation: Outlier Detection¶

Detecting outliers in the data is extremely important in the data preprocessing step of any analysis. The presence of outliers can often skew results which take into consideration these data points. There are many "rules of thumb" for what constitutes an outlier in a dataset. Here, we will use Tukey's Method for identfying outliers: An outlier step is calculated as 1.5 times the interquartile range (IQR). A data point with a feature that is beyond an outlier step outside of the IQR for that feature is considered abnormal.

In the code block below, you will need to implement the following:

  • Assign the value of the 25th percentile for the given feature to Q1. Use np.percentile for this.
  • Assign the value of the 75th percentile for the given feature to Q3. Again, use np.percentile.
  • Assign the calculation of an outlier step for the given feature to step.
  • Optionally remove data points from the dataset by adding indices to the outliers list.

NOTE: If you choose to remove any outliers, ensure that the sample data does not contain any of these points!
Once you have performed this implementation, the dataset will be stored in the variable good_data.

In the absence of an a priori reason to believe that an error in recording, processing, transmission, or some very peculiar, disruptive circumstances arose that are somehow fundamentally different from the processes repsonsible for generating the rest of the data, we have no reason to arbitrarily discard even these points.

Implementation: Dimensionality Reduction¶

When using principal component analysis, one of the main goals is to reduce the dimensionality of the data in effect, reducing the complexity of the problem. Dimensionality reduction comes at a cost: Fewer dimensions used implies less of the total variance in the data is being explained. Because of this, the cumulative explained variance ratio is extremely important for knowing how many dimensions are necessary for the problem. Companies will often run A/B tests when making small changes to their products or services to determine whether making that change will affect its customers positively or negatively. The wholesale distributor is considering changing its delivery service from currently 5 days a week to 3 days a week. However, the distributor will only make this change in delivery service for customers that react positively. How can the wholesale distributor use the customer segments to determine which customers, if any, would reach positively to the change in delivery service?
Hint: Can we assume the change affects all customers equally? How can we determine which group of customers it affects the most?

Answer: By using segments identified above, building use-cases for establishments in each of these segments based on further market research, and determining from those analyses how those changes would likely effect the supply chains of the respective establishments.

Segment 1, with its heavier reliance on perishables, would seem more likely to be effected negatively by such a change, as they would have to find ways of holding or storing perishables for longer in-between deliveries.

Segment 0, with more of an emphasis on non-perishables and dairy, seems unlikely to be affected negatively by this change, as the goods they primarily focus on don't rely on fast shipment trips for salability.

Either of the segments may welcome fewer days per week of delivery acceptance/inventory input operations, so customers' reactions would seem to be a matter of the negative or cost side of this proposed change.

To carry out A/B testing for this or any other customer-category-based change, the distributor could assign customers from each segment to test the new delivery service. They could then record the response within each segment, to see if either segment tends to react strongly negatively or positively.

If a strong pattern does emerge within a segment, the change can be rolled out to that segment if the reaction is positive, or reverted to the previous state for that segment if the reaction is negative. The A/B testing itself could thus cut across both segments and be carried out simultaneously for both segments, but the results could still be handled carefully to respond to the unique response of each segment. Also, to minimize the impact of negative effects of the change, the test group (composed of members of both segments) could be kept suitably small (but still representative) and chosen at random.
